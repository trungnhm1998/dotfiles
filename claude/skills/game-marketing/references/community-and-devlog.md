# Community Building & Devlogs

## Platform Strategy (pick one primary, then expand)

| Platform | Best for | Content format |
|---|---|---|
| **Twitter/X** | Real-time gamedev community (#screenshotsaturday, #indiedev) | GIFs, short takes, WIP screenshots |
| **TikTok / YouTube Shorts** | Broad discovery; viral potential | 15–60s dev process clips, game footage |
| **YouTube** | Long-form devlogs; evergreen content | 5–20 min devlogs, timelapse, postmortems |
| **Reddit** | High-intent audience (r/indiegaming, genre subs) | GIFs + context posts; avoid pure promotion |
| **Discord** | Engaged community; direct feedback | Server for fans, playtesters, announcements |

**Recommendation for solo dev:** Start with **Twitter/X** (lowest friction, best gamedev community overlap) + **one short-video platform** (TikTok or Shorts for discovery). Add a Discord once you have 100+ followers to give them a home.

## Devlog Content That Works

**What converts to wishlists / followers:**
- Before/after comparisons (rough → polished, placeholder art → final)
- "How I made X" — process transparency; explains a specific feature or mechanic
- Milestone moments (first playable, first playtester reaction, Steam page live)
- Honest struggle ("this mechanic wasn't fun, here's how I fixed it")
- Short gameplay GIFs (the game running, not just screenshots)

**What doesn't convert:**
- Pure announcement posts with no accompanying content ("I'm making a game!")
- Long text-only posts on visual platforms
- Posts with no call-to-action (even a "link in bio" is better than nothing)
- Posting only at launch — consistency over the months before matters more

## #ScreenshotSaturday

Post every Saturday with `#ScreenshotSaturday` and `#indiedev`. Share a WIP screenshot or GIF.

- Volume: even a small improvement is worth posting.
- Engagement: reply to other devs' posts. The community is reciprocal.
- Consistency: 6 months of weekly posts builds a visible track record for press when they research the game.

## Devlog Cadence

| Frequency | Platform | Format |
|---|---|---|
| Weekly | Twitter/X | Screenshot Saturday + any notable progress |
| Monthly | YouTube / Blog | Longer devlog covering the month's work |
| Milestone-based | All platforms | Feature reveal, demo release, Steam page launch, etc. |

**Avoid:** posting when you have nothing to show just to keep up frequency. Authentic content beats filler.

## Building a Discord Community

Don't open a Discord until you have an audience to invite (≥ 200–500 followers or a demo release). An empty Discord is a ghost town.

**Minimum channels for an indie game Discord:**
- `#announcements` (read-only)
- `#general`
- `#devlog` (you post updates; fans react)
- `#bug-reports`
- `#suggestions`

Keep it small. A tight, active community of 200 is more valuable than a quiet server of 2 000.

## Press Outreach Basics

**Who to reach out to:** Gaming journalists at small/mid outlets who cover your genre, and content creators (YouTubers, Twitch streamers) with 5k–100k subscribers in your niche. Big creators often ignore unknown devs; smaller ones are more likely to cover.

**Template (3 sentences):**
> "Hey [Name], I'm making [one-line description]. Here's a GIF/screenshot: [image]. If you'd like a review key, just reply — no obligation."

- **Timing:** 2–4 weeks before launch (they need production time).
- **Follow-up:** one follow-up 1 week later; then stop.
- **Track it:** a simple spreadsheet: name, channel, email, sent date, response.

## Authenticity Over Production Value

Indie game audiences reward transparency. Showing the real development process — struggles, failed attempts, iteration — builds trust and an invested audience. You don't need a professional video rig or a marketing team. A GIF of something new working, with an honest caption, outperforms a polished but impersonal announcement post.
